SHOW SUMMARY:

The Opie and Anthony Show broadcast opened with a heated discussion about the aftermath of a physical altercation between comedian Jay Moore and a large audience member, nicknamed "Fat Guy," outside their studio.  Video footage of the fight had circulated online, sparking considerable listener reaction, some praising it as "the best damn radio I've ever heard."  Jay Moore himself called in, detailing his version of events, expressing frustration over his stolen hat, and describing how the incident became a running gag in his subsequent comedy show.  The conversation was punctuated by frequent use of homophobic slurs and other offensive language.  Another caller claimed to possess additional video footage and Moore's Milwaukee Bucks cap.  The hosts also revealed their stressful financial situation, detailing a tense phone call with their accountant about a $19,000 debt and expressing their frustration with the syndication process, which they described as "psychological torture."

The show then transitioned into their "Most Offensive Song Contest."  The hosts promoted upcoming appearances by comedian Stephen Lynch and a planned party, while continuing to discuss the Jay Moore altercation, including accusations of drug use and other inappropriate behavior against Moore.  Buddy Hacky was mentioned as being present at Opie's house.  The contest itself featured a barrage of listener-submitted songs with extremely offensive lyrics targeting racism, homophobia, and pedophilia.  Songs like "I'm Horny for Little Girls" were played repeatedly, alongside a John Lennon parody that was interrupted by a commercial.  Anthony's brother, Joe, even submitted his own entry.  Callers reacted with a wide spectrum of responses, ranging from uproarious laughter to deep disgust.  The hosts also promoted Paisano's Italian restaurant and Rockaway Bedding.

The contest continued, with callers expressing varying degrees of shock and amusement, and one even claiming to have vomited.  One caller, James, criticized the lack of originality in the songs, while another threatened to deny paternity if he was the subject of one.  A call from Kevin at Fox News requested audio/video of the Jay Moore studio brawl, highlighting Fox News' comparatively relaxed broadcasting standards.  The hosts mentioned a potentially un-airable topic that had caused them concern.  The contest reached a climax with the submission of "Mein Fuhrer," a highly offensive song about the Holocaust, prompting intense outrage from listeners, particularly those of Jewish heritage.  Callers like David (whose grandparents were Holocaust victims) and Andrew (identifying as both Jewish and Cuban) expressed their deep offense. The intense negative reaction forced the hosts to end the contest prematurely.

Following the controversy, the show briefly shifted to a prank call made to a drunk "Psycho Mark" at Shea Stadium on opening day, incorporating listener accounts of opening day shenanigans, and a discussion about Mariah Carey's illness and its graphic implications.  The remainder of the segment consisted of continued fallout from the "most offensive song" contest, along with more listener reactions.

The final segment focused on a frantic search for a missing commercial ("PIX spot") that a producer, "Spaz" (Mark), had failed to air.  Spaz, heavily intoxicated after a Mets game, faced repeated berating from Opie and Anthony for his negligence.  The segment was filled with profanity and escalating tension as the hosts investigated the missing commercial, involving speculation about other staff members' roles, including "Earl" and their boss, "Jeremy."  Checking production logs (code 109 was mentioned) became part of the search.  The hosts played audio clips of Spaz's severely slurred speech from a public service announcement and a community calendar segment, described as "torture" to listen to.  Numerous calls from people who were with Spaz at the Mets game corroborated his intoxicated state.  Spaz also provided various drunk impressions during the segment.  The segment further interwove listener calls from the "most offensive song" contest, showcasing more bizarre and offensive entries, and concluded with a clip of Casey Kasem's frustrated rant about difficult transitions, juxtaposed with Spaz's drunken performance. Cowbell Bill was also mentioned.
