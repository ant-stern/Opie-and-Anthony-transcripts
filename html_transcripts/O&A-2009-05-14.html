The Opie and Anthony Show broadcast opened with a jarring juxtaposition:  a song about Major Tom interspersed with chaotic chatter about money and a potential car fire.  The hosts then abruptly shifted to a discussion about childhood dioramas,  jokingly evolving into a listener contest for Opie and Anthony-themed dioramas and other elementary school projects like macaroni creations,  with one caller even suggesting a macaroni AK-47.  

The show then welcomed Dr. Michael Baden, a forensic pathologist, and a colleague (later identified as British coroner Peter Dean) for an extensive interview covering his career highlights, including autopsies of John F. Kennedy and Sid Vicious.  The discussion ranged widely across forensic pathology: challenges in determining cause of death, particularly in suffocation cases; the difficulties in prosecuting serial killers;  the limitations of forensic science as portrayed in media; the JFK assassination; TWA Flight 800; determining time of death; and anecdotes from Dr. Baden's career, often employing frank and darkly humorous language. They also discussed the surprisingly long preservation of bodies in extremely cold conditions, citing a 1930s plane crash victim as an example. A caller's question about cryogenics led to a comparison of U.S. and other countries' coroner systems, and Peter Dean shared experiences investigating deaths of British citizens abroad, particularly in Kenya, touching upon the Lockerbie bombing and the use of fat emboli to determine if victims were alive during the crash. The coroner's roles in investigating deaths, even those outside their jurisdiction, were explained, along with their professional relationship, including co-authoring books with Dr. Baden’s wife, and anecdotes about their experiences with passive-aggressive behavior during this process.  Famous cases like O.J. Simpson, Robert Blake, Klaus von Bülow, and Terri Schiavo were analyzed, highlighting forensic aspects and legal battles.  Dr. Baden’s involvement in the Phil Spector trial (under a gag order) and the Billy Martin death investigation were also mentioned.  The discussion included humorous anecdotes about unusual requests (exhuming Shakespeare, DNA testing of royal remains) and misconceptions (sewing up anuses in the morgue),  along with speculation about the nature of coroner parties. A caller's question about organ transplants prompted a discussion about organ allocation and ethical considerations.  Finally, Peter Dean shared his hobby of photographing tattoos on the deceased, noting regional and subcultural differences in styles.  A superfan, Sam, had a highly enthusiastic, albeit somewhat awkward, interview with Rob Zombie.
